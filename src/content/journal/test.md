---
title: "Seasonal Depression, But Tropical"
date: "2026-05-28"
draft: true
---

I used to think seasonal depression belonged to countries with actual winters.

Places with grey skies by four in the afternoon. Radiators. Snow accumulating quietly outside apartment windows. Scandinavian novels. The aesthetics of emotional deterioration seemed geographically specific somehow.

Meanwhile, Hanoi in the summer feels aggressively alive.

Everything sweats. Plastic chairs soften under heat. Motorbike engines radiate through entire intersections. Fruit rots faster. The air itself begins to feel overused.

And yet I increasingly suspect that prolonged heat produces its own form of seasonal psychological distortion.

Not sadness exactly.

More like cognitive exhaustion.

The strange inability to fully begin anything. Attention becoming viscous. Opening the same tabs repeatedly without reading them. Moving between documents without retaining much. Existing permanently in a state slightly adjacent to concentration.

Summer here often feels less like a season and more like low-level atmospheric interference.

Even language changes texture in this weather. Emails become shorter. Conversations flatten. Thoughts arrive slower and dissolve faster. Entire afternoons disappear into heat management strategies: iced coffee, cold showers, rearranged working hours, lying motionless under weak electric fans pretending to read.

There is also something oddly lonely about tropical exhaustion because it rarely looks cinematic.

Winter sadness has an established visual culture. Tropical exhaustion mostly looks like slightly deteriorated productivity.

A person sitting in front of a laptop at 3PM, surrounded by unfinished tabs, unable to decide whether they are researching, procrastinating, or simply overheating.
